Sound and Sentiment

A Necessary Conflict

Electronic Music Portfolio - Elisabetta Marvulli

Introduction

This portfolio represents a journey through my experiences and discoveries in the field of electroacoustic music during my studies at the Turin Conservatory. In each chapter, I have sought to explore, through the described projects, the relationship between artistic expression and sound processing technologies studied in recent years.

One of the most recurring themes in the works presented in this portfolio is my conflictual relationship with sound, due to a feeling of inadequacy that has always accompanied me. This feeling drives me to want to express myself through music, but at the same time leads me to fear it.

Starting this course of study was an unconscious attempt on my part to obtain the expressive tools necessary to allow this feeling to be released. Each project explores how the integration between instrumental writing and electronic processing can become a means to explore and communicate the most profound human experiences.

The compilation of this portfolio has been an opportunity to document the results of my creative and technical efforts, but also to stimulate deeper reflections on the possible future of my artistic practice within the context of contemporary sound experience.

Everything Freezes in the Water

A composition for voice and live electronics developed for quadraphonic diffusion system, created in the context of Composition III course during the 2023/2024 academic year.

Figure 1.1: SOPHIE, "Oil Of Every Pearl's Un-Insides" - The album cover that inspired the composition

The Voice

In this composition, the focus of my research was the voice. The choice of this instrument derives not only from its universality and intrinsic capacity to express deep emotions, but also from the challenge of translating these emotions into a language that I emotionally perceive as complex and difficult, but from which I cannot separate myself for my music.

Inspiration

The inspiration for the composition comes from a track by SOPHIE, "Is It Cold in the Water", whose lyrics I rewrote with the intent to explore the artist's words and evolve the narrative thread of the song, while paying homage to the British producer's legacy in terms of musical innovation.

Setup

Figure 1.2: Electroacoustic chain for Everything Freezes in the Water

For voice recording, a Neumann U87 was used, known for its classic smooth timbre. The speakers are arranged in a quadraphonic setup at a height of about 170cm from the ground.

Figure 1.3: Monitor positioning for Everything Freezes in the Water

Live Electronics

Much of the real-time digital sound processing revolves around the use of filter banks for spectral analysis of the voice. The live electronics part is managed through two patches created in the Max/MSP environment.

Figure 1.4: Max/MSP schema showing internal connections between various modules

Patch A Modules

1. VOCODER: Implemented using Max's native "mcs.fffb" object with 256 bandpass filters. The first bank consists of filters tuned starting from 60 Hz with major second intervals.

2. FILTERED WHITE NOISE: Uses white noise as base sound applying bandpass filters to modify the resulting spectrum, using spectral analysis data from the vocoder to decide which frequencies to cut.

3. GRANULATOR: Real-time granulation with 5 parameters: FREQ (buffer reading frequency), DENSITY (grain density), WHERE (buffer reading point), SPEED (grain playback speed), WIN (buffer reading window).

4. ONSET SPATIALIZATION: Spatializes granulation across four speakers based on voice transients detected by fluid.transientslice~.

5. WAVESET: Uses a gen patch where the rate parameter is modified to determine processing speed and sample playback corresponding to the voice signal.

6. HARMONIZER: Created through IRCAM's psychoirtrist~ object. Frequencies are determined through a harmonic series from the sung fundamental, identified by a pitch descriptor.

Figure 1.6: Spatialization module based on transient detection

An Index of Metals Production - TLT 2024

A video-opera by Fausto Romitelli for soprano, ensemble, electronics and multi-projection, with texts by Kenka Lekovich and videos by Alessandro Romoli and Paolo Pachini.

Figure 2.1: Ictus Ensemble during the performance

Introduction

An Index of Metals is a multimedia spectacle where live music, video projections, and electronic music that blends and alternates with live performance are interwoven. It breaks with the conventions of traditional musical performance, inviting the audience to experience an immersive experience.

"The original suggestion derives from a Roy Lichtenstein painting, in which a girl is drowning among the waves of a cold and colorless sea, which Romitelli's eye saw as liquid metal rather than salt water. From here, imagination led the author to the free-style story of 'the assimilation of a girl by the world of machines'."

Ensemble & P.A.

The ensemble predetermined by Fausto Romitelli consists of 11 instruments plus electronics:

  • Flute (also Bass Flute, Piccolo, Guitar pitch pipe)
  • Oboe (also English Horn, Guitar pitch pipe)
  • Clarinet in Bb (also Bass Clarinet, Harmonica)
  • Trumpet in C (also Harmonica)
  • Trombone (also Harmonica)
  • Voice (with megaphone)
  • Piano (also Sampler)
  • Violin
  • Viola
  • Cello
  • Electronics

Figure 2.2: Stage plan for AltreVoci Ensemble showing instrument positioning

Technical Implementation

Given the complexity of the opera, it was fundamental to identify a system that would allow connecting and enabling communication between the live electronics part and the instruments connected to it. Using a predominantly digital cabling system (Dante Protocol) at the Conservatory, the challenge was creating a matrix capable of handling all necessary channels.

Figure 2.3: Dante matrix implementation for signal management

Audio Systems

Two speaker systems were employed:

  • Frontal PA System: Two speakers (Stereo L/R) positioned at stage level
  • Surround Amplification System: Eight diffusers arranged around the audience: two frontal, four on the sides, and two toward the back of the hall

Tape & Electronics

The electroacoustic part consists of tapes and real-time audio manipulations. Audio files are activated at predetermined points in the score by the Cue Man, represented by members of the computer team. These files have two main musical functions: enriching and blending the timbre and harmony of what is played by the ensemble, and creating a series of "electroacoustic interludes".

Figure 2.4: Input patch configuration on Midas mixer

Live Electronics Processing

The computer team analyzed the score and Ictus Ensemble recordings to identify points for live treatments, implementing:

  • Distortion on strings through Max/MSP
  • Delay with feedback via Max/MSP
  • General reverb on mix and individual instruments using FdnReverb
  • Voice and string modulation through Max/MSP
  • Kontakt patches for the sampler

Figure 2.5: Complete cabling schema for An Index of Metals

Microphone Setup

Microphones were divided into two categories: amplification and recording. All instruments have specific amplification microphones, with additional recording microphones on selected instruments. Strings are amplified using DPA 4061 while recording signals come from Schoeps mk4, allowing linear recording while avoiding feedback during hall amplification.

EveryBagel

An acousmatic compositional experience inspired by the film "Everything Everywhere All At Once" (2022), a fantasy work that addresses profound themes related to daily life and interpersonal relationships, particularly the mother-daughter relationship.

Inspiration

The emotional bond that develops in the film's narrative pushed me to musically explore the scenes that best represent this relationship through sampling of audio files extracted from the most significant moments of the film, particularly those containing dialogues between the protagonists.

All sound objects used were created by sampling various scenes from the film in different ways, with a specific focus on dialogues between the two characters. From the sampling of these, I derived a wide variety of sound objects with which I developed my compositional processes.

Electronic Processes

The basic structure of the composition is founded on drones derived from some of the most "musical" scenes of the film. On these drones, I built gestures characterized mainly by clusters of different density, composed of small vocal fragments, both in "clean" version and altered through pitch shifting and timestretching techniques.

Processing Techniques

For processing these audio samples, I used various Max patches:

1. Convolution Patches

Used for drone sections, where I convolved various scenes together, subsequently performing stretching both with Paulstretch and directly in Reaper.

2. Spectral Freeze

For the more melodic parts overlaying the drones, I used a Spectral Freeze patch, selecting specific spectral windows and reproducing them randomly.

3. Vocal Processing

For vocal parts, I also adopted the same spectral freeze patch. In some sections, clusters were obtained through granulation, while in others I manually overlaid small vocal samples in Reaper to stratify and densify the various micro-vocal extracts with greater precision.

Convolution is a cross-synthesis method that combines two audio sources such that, in the frequency domain, frequencies they have in common are emphasized proportionally and those they don't share are minimized. In the time domain, how these frequencies move, spread, and decay is another part of the convolution process.

The Well Tuned Guitar

The Techniques II course gave me the opportunity to further explore an instrument already familiar to me: the guitar. By experimenting with a vast set of techniques applied to the acoustic instrument, I trained not only in seeking to produce a study using exclusively the learned gestures, but this also forced me to question how to transcribe the processes obtained through these techniques onto sheet music.

Extended Techniques Description

During months of study, I identified mainly ten different excitation techniques, especially through the use of objects positioned in various ways on the guitar neck. The idea behind these techniques was to create sounds different from those typical of the guitar, while still seeking to maintain its timbral identity, without making it too obvious.

Ten Techniques Used

  1. KotoLikeBend: Place pencil or similar between strings and neck toward 7th fret, press strings beyond pencil and pluck
  2. MetalNeck: Play rapid glissandos with any cylindrical metal object placed on strings, alternating these glissandos with pizzicatos
  3. ChordSlap: Play chords by striking the guitar with hand toward the bridge
  4. Stretch: Scrape the string starting from mid-neck toward the bridge
  5. BodySlap: With guitar resting on legs, percuss the body with hand
  6. PencilSlap: Play chords or single strings by striking them with a pencil
  7. PencilRemoved: Quickly remove pencil positioned under strings, letting open strings resonate
  8. BagFry: Get aluminum bag, position between neck and strings toward first frets, pluck open strings so they touch bag surface
  9. Arpeggi: Play notes shown in tablature fingerpicking style
  10. PencilShift: Play guitar resting on legs and from KOTO position rapidly move pencil between frets

Alternative Tunings

Besides using alternative methods to play the instrument, I also sought to use tunings different from typical guitar tuning. Two tunings were used for this study, both inspired by two artists: Ben Howard and La Monte Young.

Ben Howard Tuning

The first tuning refers to many tracks by the English artist, with strings tuned as follows (from sixth string): C-G-C-G-G-C.

La Monte Young Just Intonation

For the second tuning, I analyzed that used by La Monte Young for "The Well Tuned Piano", based on Just Intonation, where musical intervals correspond to ratios of whole numbers of frequencies. After various combination attempts among different pitches, I chose to use 6 notes: 74 Hz D#, 83 Hz F, 97 Hz G, 192 Hz G#, 223 Hz A#, 260 Hz C.

Score Description

The notation chosen is that commonly used for guitar: tablature. Each extended technique is associated with a symbol, placed at the beginning of the staff line relative to its voice. The score includes a subdivision of extended techniques into voices, each with its respective symbol.

Figure 4.12: Score excerpt for The Well Tuned Guitar showing technique symbols and tablature notation

Given the simultaneous use of different tunings for the gestures, the piece was conceived for tape and live instrument, where tape parts correspond to recordings of one or more voices described in the score, thus allowing choice of what to play live at the performer's discretion.

Pulsar Synthesis System

A Pulsar Synthesis system developed in Max/MSP in collaboration with Aniello Maffettone, based on Curtis Roads' concepts described in his book "Microsound". Pulsar synthesis (PS), named after rotating neutron stars that emit periodic signals between 0.25 Hz and 642 Hz, is a powerful digital sound synthesis method.

Curtis Roads' Theory

As described by Curtis Roads: "Pulsar synthesis generates electronic impulses and tones similar to those produced by analog instruments like the Ondioline and Hohner Elektronium, designed around the principle of altered impulse trains. In its advanced form, pulsar synthesis generates a world of cross-sampled and rhythmically structured sounds."

"If the speed of the beat is gradually increased beyond the time constant of the filter and the limits beyond which the ear can no longer distinguish, what began as a rhythmically repeated note becomes continuous... We witness a continuous transition between what might be called duration intervals characterized as rhythmic intervals and duration intervals characterized as pitch levels." - Stockhausen (1955)

Musical Hierarchy

To better understand this relationship, it's necessary to know the musical hierarchy used by Roads for temporal classification of sound events:

  1. Infinite: Ideal time range of mathematical durations
  2. Supra: Time scale beyond that of a single composition (months, years, decades)
  3. Macro: Overall time scale of musical architecture or form (minutes or hours)
  4. Meso: Form divisions, groupings of sound objects in hierarchies (minutes or seconds)
  5. Sound Object: Basic unit of musical structure, generalizing the traditional note concept
  6. Micro: Sound particles on a time scale extending to the threshold of auditory perception (milliseconds)
  7. Sample: Atomic level of digital audio systems (thousandths of seconds)
  8. Subsample: Fluctuations on too brief a time scale to be adequately recorded or perceived
  9. Infinitesimal: Ideal time range of mathematical durations

Pulsar Anatomy

A single impulse consists of two fundamental parts: a waveform (w), called pulsaret, with a period (d) (duty cycle), followed by a silence interval (s). The total duration of an impulse (p) is therefore p = d + s, where w corresponds to the waveform, p to the pulsar period, d to the duty cycle, and s to silence.

Figure 5.1: Anatomy of a Pulsar showing waveform (w), duty cycle (d), silence (s), and total period (p)

System Parameters

Waveform Options

  • Sinusoidal
  • Multicycle sinusoid
  • Band-limited impulse
  • Decaying multicycle sinusoid
  • Cosmic pulsar waveforms from neutron star Vela X-1

Envelope Types

  • Linear Decay/Attack
  • Exponential Decay/Attack
  • FOF (Fonction d'Onde Formantique)
  • Rectangular, Triangle
  • Hamming, Hanning, Blackman, Welch, Kaiser

Burst Masking

Generates regular pulsation patterns interrupted at regular intervals. These intervals can be defined through an on/off model, resulting in a burst ratio (b:r), where b is burst length in pulsation periods and r is rest length in pulsation periods.

Figure 5.2: Typical pulsaret waveforms available in the system

Figure 5.3: Various envelope types showing their effect on spectral content

Max/MSP Implementation

The patch was developed with specific modules for waveform selection, each pulsaret having main parameters allowing personalization: frequency, n-periods for different waveforms (Cosine, Sine, Sinc, Decaying multicycle sinusoid), and envelope type.

Figure 5.4: Pulsar Synthesis Patch in Max/MSP showing the complete interface

Technical Implementation

The algorithm developed for waveform selection is based on creating specific buffers for each pulsaret module. By sending messages containing waveform type and cycle number information to each buffer, it's possible to define each pulsaret in terms of samples (2048).

The reproduction of different pulsars was made polyphonic through coding within the mc.poly~ system, managing 5 voices unified in a single multichannel signal. Use of this native Max/MSP object allows up- or down-sampling of the patch audio output, which is interesting from a spectral point of view.
